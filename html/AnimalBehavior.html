Because animals are unable to communicate with us through a verbal language, we have to rely on our understanding of their behavior through body language and vocalization recognition. With dogs, a lot of their communication involves the position of their tails. However, you cannot always rely on just one body part. By taking into account facial muscles, breathing patterns, posture, and more, I can more accurately understand what a dog is feeling at any given moment.

Today's dog is a result of breeding for hundreds of years and consequently has brought them further from their wild counterparts. Despite your dog being incapable of surviving in the wild, they still enjoy performing natural behaviors like foraging. Designing enrichment tools that allow dogs to use their nose and paws searching for treats encourages mental stimulation. Other ways to boost mental stimulation include obstacle courses, much like those seen at dog agility competitions. As a trainer, I lay out a trail of challenges and teach the dog to work their way through it. Some aspects of the course contain unstable platforms that may invoke anxiety. Building trust and confidence in the dog to perform these otherwise daunting tasks provides enrichment.

As a trainer, I co-instructed both a training course for puppies less than a year old and dogs aged two to five years old to learn basic commands such as sit, down, and leave it. Training goes far beyond working with just the animal. I would argue that being a dog trainer requires excellent communication skills with the owner. After all, if training techniques are not consistent, a dog is incapable of grasping the concept we are attempting to train them to do. For example, a common issue among many dogs is the ability to walk on a leash without pulling. If a dog trainer works with a dog to prevent this undesirable behavior, but the owner allows their dog to continue tugging on the leash, then the dog will have difficulty understanding that it is not supposed to pull. Teaching an owner the importance of this consistency and equipping them with science-based positive reinforcement training methods is crucial to the educational aspect of being an animal trainer.
